When I first started teaching Computer Science, I noticed a recurring pattern among students: those who could ace theoretical exams often struggled when faced with a blank IDE and a real-world problem. 

The gap between understanding a concept *in theory* (like how a Linked List works or the definition of polymorphism) and applying it *in practice* (building a feature for an app) is wider than most realize.

### The Illusion of Competence

Reading a textbook or watching a tutorial gives you the illusion of competence. It feels like you're learning, but you're actually just consuming information. **True learning happens when you get stuck.**

When you build a side project, you encounter edge cases that textbooks gloss over:
- How do you handle authentication securely?
- What happens when your API rate-limits you?
- How do you deploy this and keep it running?

### My Advice: Start Small, Iterate Fast

You don't need to build the next Facebook. Build something that solves a small problem for yourself:
1. A habit tracker CLI tool
2. A script that scrapes your university schedule
3. A simple portfolio website like this one

The act of taking an idea from zero to deployed will teach you more about software engineering than a semester of theoretical coursework. **Don't wait for permission or the perfect idea. Just start building.**
